Great Figures of the Ming

This page is intentionally complete. Each featured figure is given name, dates, office, achievement, and a stable position in history.

Canonical records

Those the Archive Remembers Clearly

The choice of figures is itself a historical argument: founders, emperors, admirals, reformers, generals, and moral officials are preserved as legible subjects.

Founding Emperor

Zhu Yuanzhang

Years
1328-1398
Achievement
Founded the Ming in 1368 and restructured imperial rule as the Hongwu Emperor.
Historical status
Founder, disciplinarian, restorer of dynastic order.
Reference
Ming Taizu shilu, founding edicts, tax and military registers.
"The dynasty begins with order."
Emperor

Zhu Di (Yongle Emperor)

Years
1360-1424
Achievement
Moved the political center to Beijing and commissioned the Forbidden City and maritime expeditions.
Historical status
Architect of imperial expansion and central spectacle.
Reference
Yongle reign records, capital construction files, court memorials.
"Empire is made visible through scale."
Admiral

Zheng He

Years
1371-1433
Achievement
Led the treasure voyages that projected Ming power across the Indian Ocean.
Historical status
Symbol of maritime reach and diplomatic theater.
Reference
Voyage inscriptions, eunuch records, tribute mission accounts.
"The fleet remembers the admiral first."
Grand Secretary

Zhang Juzheng

Years
1525-1582
Achievement
Advanced fiscal and administrative reforms during the Wanli era.
Historical status
Elite reformer and manager of state capacity.
Reference
Memorial collections, examination records, reform papers.
"Reform appears under one name; its burden falls on many."
General

Qi Jiguang

Years
1528-1588
Achievement
Commanded anti-pirate campaigns and frontier defenses; authored military manuals.
Historical status
Model commander of disciplined defense.
Reference
Campaign reports, Jixiao Xinshu, border troop records.
"Defense preserves the realm, but soldiers rarely keep the narrative."
Moral Official

Hai Rui

Years
1514-1587
Achievement
Became a late-Ming emblem of moral criticism, clean administration, and fearless remonstrance.
Historical status
Austere conscience of officialdom.
Reference
Memorials, local administrative records, later moral biographies.
"Integrity is easy to monumentalize after the fact."
